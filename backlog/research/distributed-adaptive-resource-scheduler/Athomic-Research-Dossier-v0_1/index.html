    Athomic Research Dossier v0.1

    Athomic: A Distributed Self-Adaptive Scheduler for Modern AI Serving Systems

    Authors

    Valter Hugo Guandaline

    Status

    Active Research Proposal

    Intended Venues

    • ACM Middleware
    • ACM Symposium on Cloud Computing (SoCC)
    • USENIX Annual Technical Conference (ATC)

    Executive Summary

    Athomic is an ongoing research effort investigating whether adaptive concurrency control, workload learning, fairness mechanisms, distributed coordination, and Service Level Objective (SLO) preservation can coexist within a unified scheduling architecture.

    Existing systems typically optimize one scheduling objective in isolation. Adaptive concurrency controllers preserve latency under overload conditions, fair schedulers improve resource allocation among tenants, AI-oriented schedulers focus on placement decisions and GPU utilization, while learned resource managers attempt to infer workload characteristics through historical observations.

    Athomic explores an alternative perspective.

    Rather than treating these concerns as independent subsystems, Athomic models them as components of a single decision-making pipeline capable of continuously adapting scheduling behavior according to workload characteristics, runtime telemetry, fairness constraints, and fleet-wide conditions.

    This document presents the scientific motivation, architectural evolution, experimental methodology, and publication strategy guiding the project.

    1. Introduction

    1.1 Motivation

    Modern AI-serving platforms are becoming increasingly difficult to operate efficiently.

    Unlike traditional web services, AI workloads exhibit highly heterogeneous execution patterns. Two requests targeting the same model may differ by several orders of magnitude in execution time, memory footprint, GPU consumption, token generation rate, and throughput requirements. Such variability challenges assumptions embedded in many existing scheduling and admission control systems.

    Adaptive concurrency controllers such as Netflix Concurrency Limits were designed primarily to preserve latency under overload conditions. Cluster schedulers such as Mesos, Ray, and Volcano focus on fair resource allocation, placement decisions, and distributed execution. More recent systems optimized for machine learning workloads introduce mechanisms for gang scheduling, GPU awareness, and workload-specific optimizations.

    These solutions are highly effective within their respective domains. However, modern AI-serving environments increasingly require several of these capabilities simultaneously.

    Expected Outcome

    Establish whether assumptions made by traditional schedulers remain valid for modern heterogeneous AI workloads.

    1.2 Problem Statement

    Consider a multi-tenant platform simultaneously offering embedding generation, Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipelines, autonomous agents, and language model inference.

    Such a platform must answer questions spanning multiple disciplines.

    Can another request be safely admitted?

    Will this request violate a latency objective?

    Is a tenant consuming more than its fair share of resources?

    Has the workload profile changed enough to justify reducing concurrency?

    Are neighboring replicas already under pressure?

    Would delaying execution improve global fleet efficiency?

    Current systems typically address only a subset of these questions.

    Adaptive concurrency controllers preserve latency.

    Fair schedulers preserve equity.

    AI-oriented schedulers optimize placement.

    Learned resource managers estimate workload behavior.

    Distributed orchestrators improve cluster-wide coordination.

    Unfortunately, these mechanisms are usually implemented independently and optimized according to different objectives.

    This raises an open research question.

    Open Research Question

    Can adaptive concurrency, workload learning, fairness, and distributed coordination coexist within a single scheduling framework without introducing prohibitive complexity or unacceptable throughput degradation?

    1.3 Why Existing Systems Are Not Enough

    Existing systems evolved in response to very different operational constraints.

    Netflix Concurrency Limits emerged from latency-sensitive microservices.

    Mesos and Dominant Resource Fairness were designed to improve fairness in large-scale datacenters.

    Volcano addresses GPU-intensive workloads and gang scheduling.

    Ray focuses on distributed execution for machine learning applications.

    DeepRM and Decima investigate whether scheduling policies themselves can become trainable.

    These systems rarely interact. Each optimizes a specific scheduling objective while assuming other concerns are handled elsewhere in the stack.

    Modern AI platforms challenge this decomposition.

    A single inference endpoint may simultaneously require latency preservation, fairness among tenants, workload-aware admission control, memory-aware scheduling, fleet-wide coordination, and graceful degradation mechanisms.

    Athomic emerged from the observation that these requirements are no longer independent.

    Impact on Athomic

    If these assumptions prove invalid for AI-serving workloads, admission control may need to evolve into a broader resource scheduling problem.

    2. Research Vision

    2.1 Long-Term Objective

    The long-term objective of Athomic is not merely to become another concurrency limiter.

    Instead, the project seeks to investigate whether adaptive concurrency control, fairness, workload characterization, distributed coordination, and Service Level Objective preservation can coexist within a single scheduling architecture.

    If successful, Athomic may occupy a design space situated between adaptive admission controllers and AI-oriented resource schedulers.

    More importantly, Athomic may provide empirical evidence that workload learning and fairness should become first-class scheduling primitives for modern AI-serving systems.

    2.2 Research Narrative

    Athomic did not originate as a scheduler.

    It emerged as an adaptive concurrency controller inspired primarily by Netflix Concurrency Limits and Gradient2.

    As new operational requirements appeared, additional mechanisms were progressively incorporated.

    Memory-aware admission control was introduced to reduce oversubscription events.

    Learned cost estimation reduced the need for manually declared workload profiles.

    Queue intelligence and fairness mechanisms were added to prevent starvation.

    Distributed budgets, fleet pressure sharing, and cooperative dampening extended scheduling decisions beyond the boundaries of a single process.

    More recently, Dominant Resource Fairness concepts were incorporated to improve equitable resource sharing among competing workloads.

    At its current stage, Athomic no longer behaves as a traditional admission controller.

    Instead, it increasingly resembles a distributed resource scheduler operating inside application runtimes.

    This observation motivates the central question of this research.

    Research Narrative

    Can a scheduler emerging organically from adaptive concurrency principles provide measurable advantages over systems specifically optimized for only one scheduling objective?

    2.3 Research Hypothesis

    Most existing adaptive admission controllers optimize decisions using isolated feedback signals such as latency, CPU utilization, or queue depth.

    This approach is often insufficient for AI-serving environments, where workloads exhibit unpredictable behavior, resource requirements are difficult to estimate, and multiple tenants compete for limited computational capacity.

    We hypothesize that integrating learned cost estimation, multi-resource fairness, queue intelligence, distributed coordination, and multi-signal adaptive admission control into a unified framework can optimize systemic trade-offs between resource efficiency and service quality.

    More specifically, we expect Athomic to simultaneously improve resource utilization, fairness, SLO preservation, failure recovery, and tail latency stability while maintaining throughput within an acceptable degradation range.

    2.4 Positioning Statement

    Athomic is a distributed self-adaptive scheduler designed for heterogeneous AI-serving workloads.

    Rather than treating admission control, fairness, workload estimation, and distributed coordination as independent concerns, Athomic models them as components of a unified decision-making process.

    This positioning motivates the broader scientific objective of the project.

    To investigate whether scheduling mechanisms historically developed in isolation can be combined into a coherent architecture capable of outperforming specialized solutions under modern AI-serving conditions.

    3. Positioning Within Existing Literature

    Athomic occupies an unusual position within the current scheduling landscape.

    Most scheduling systems were designed to optimize a single objective and were developed within relatively isolated research communities. Adaptive concurrency controllers emerged from latency-sensitive microservice architectures. Fair schedulers evolved primarily from resource management problems in shared clusters and datacenters. AI-oriented schedulers appeared much later, driven by GPU-intensive workloads, large-scale training pipelines, and distributed inference services. More recently, learned resource management systems introduced the idea that scheduling policies themselves may become trainable components.

    Athomic attempts to bridge these traditionally disconnected domains.

    Rather than optimizing a single scheduling objective, Athomic investigates whether mechanisms such as adaptive admission control, workload learning, fairness, queue intelligence, and distributed coordination can be modeled as components of a common scheduling abstraction.

    This positioning naturally raises a broader question.

    Are these mechanisms fundamentally independent, or do they represent different manifestations of a larger scheduling problem emerging from the increasing complexity of AI-serving systems?

    3.2 What Existing Systems Optimize

    Most production scheduling systems optimize only a subset of the dimensions relevant to AI-serving workloads.

    Adaptive concurrency controllers primarily attempt to preserve latency and maintain throughput under overload conditions.

    Fair schedulers attempt to maximize equitable resource sharing while avoiding starvation.

    AI-native schedulers focus on placement decisions, GPU allocation, and gang scheduling.

    Learned resource managers attempt to infer workload behavior and optimize scheduling policies through historical observations.

    Athomic differs from these approaches by attempting to optimize several of these dimensions simultaneously.

    4. Research Landscape

    The objective of this chapter is not simply to summarize previous work.

    Instead, we seek to understand the assumptions embedded within existing systems, determine whether those assumptions remain valid for modern AI-serving environments, and precisely position Athomic relative to the current state of the art.

    At the conclusion of this investigation, we expect to answer four questions.

    How do existing systems detect congestion?

    How do they distribute resources fairly?

    How do they model workload uncertainty?

    Which capabilities remain largely unexplored?

    4.1 Adaptive Concurrency Systems

    Motivation

    Adaptive concurrency mechanisms represent the intellectual foundation upon which Athomic was originally built.

    These systems are primarily concerned with preserving latency under overload conditions while maximizing throughput. Most of them assume that latency degradation is an adequate proxy for resource saturation and rely on feedback loops to dynamically adjust concurrency levels.

    Systems Under Investigation

    Netflix Concurrency Limits will be studied primarily because of Gradient2, Vegas, and latency-driven adaptation strategies.

    Envoy Adaptive Concurrency provides an alternative perspective centered around service meshes and request-level congestion avoidance.

    Additional mechanisms inspired by networking congestion control algorithms may also be investigated.

    Expected Outcome

    At the conclusion of this investigation, we expect to determine whether Athomic's current adaptation mechanisms already approximate the state of the art or whether stronger control-theoretic approaches should be considered.

    Impact on Athomic

    A deeper understanding of convergence properties and stability guarantees may motivate replacing heuristic adaptation mechanisms with more formally grounded controllers.

    4.2 Fair Scheduling

    Motivation

    As Athomic evolved, admission decisions became increasingly influenced by fairness considerations.

    At this point, the system no longer decides only whether a request should execute.

    It also attempts to determine which request deserves access to scarce resources.

    Systems Under Investigation

    Dominant Resource Fairness remains the most influential work in this area and will be studied extensively.

    Mesos provides a practical implementation of DRF.

    Kubernetes API Priority and Fairness introduces ideas related to tenant isolation, quota management, and hierarchical prioritization.

    Tromino extends DRF by incorporating queue demand into scheduling decisions.

    Expected Outcome

    This investigation should reveal whether Athomic's current fairness mechanisms already approximate existing models or whether stronger mathematical guarantees can be achieved.

    Future Investigation

    Explore fleet-wide implementations of Dominant Resource Fairness capable of operating under eventually consistent distributed environments.

    4.3 AI-oriented Scheduling

    Motivation

    Artificial Intelligence workloads introduce challenges that are rarely encountered in traditional cloud applications.

    Resource consumption becomes highly variable, GPUs emerge as first-class scheduling constraints, and tasks may require coordinated execution across multiple devices.

    Inference requests, embedding generation, fine-tuning jobs, and autonomous agents frequently compete for the same infrastructure.

    Systems Under Investigation

    Ray will be analyzed because of its resource-aware execution model and placement groups.

    Volcano offers insights into hierarchical queues, gang scheduling, and GPU-aware resource allocation.

    Pollux, Gandiva, and Tiresias provide alternative approaches designed specifically for machine learning clusters.

    Expected Outcome

    At the end of this study, we expect to determine whether Athomic should remain an adaptive scheduler embedded inside application runtimes or gradually evolve toward cluster-wide orchestration.

    5. Research Questions

    The experimental campaign described in this document is guided by four complementary questions.

    Together, these questions attempt to determine whether Athomic should continue evolving as an adaptive concurrency framework or whether it already behaves as a new category of scheduler.

    5.1 Multi-signal Adaptation

    Adaptive admission controllers usually rely on one or two feedback signals.

    Latency-based algorithms monitor response times.

    Memory-aware systems observe resource consumption.

    Queue managers focus primarily on waiting time.

    Athomic proposes an alternative approach by simultaneously incorporating latency, throughput, queue pressure, workload costs, error rates, fleet-wide congestion signals, and fairness indicators.

    The first research question investigates whether combining these signals leads to more effective scheduling decisions.

    RQ1

    Can multi-signal adaptive admission control improve SLO preservation under heterogeneous AI workloads?

    5.2 Learning Resource Consumption

    Most schedulers assume that workloads already know how much CPU, memory, or GPU capacity they require.

    This assumption rarely holds in AI-serving environments.

    Two requests sent to the same model may consume drastically different amounts of memory and execution time.

    Athomic attempts to learn workload characteristics dynamically and use these estimates to influence future admission decisions.

    RQ2

    Can online cost estimation reduce oversubscription while improving resource utilization?

    5.3 Fairness Beyond Local Decisions

    Traditional adaptive concurrency controllers are largely agnostic to fairness.

    Conversely, fairness-oriented schedulers generally ignore latency preservation.

    Athomic attempts to bridge these worlds.

    Distributed budgets, fleet pressure sharing, and Dominant Resource Fairness mechanisms introduce notions of fairness into an otherwise latency-sensitive scheduler.

    RQ3

    Can distributed fairness mechanisms preserve equitable resource allocation without sacrificing throughput?

    5.4 Unifying Scheduling Objectives

    Perhaps the most ambitious aspect of this work is the assumption that scheduling objectives traditionally optimized independently may coexist within a single architecture.

    This assumption remains largely unexplored in current literature.

    Athomic provides an opportunity to experimentally evaluate this hypothesis.

    RQ4

    Can a unified scheduling framework outperform specialized systems targeting only concurrency control, fairness, or overload protection?

    6. Athomic Architecture

    This chapter describes Athomic not merely as a software artifact, but as a scheduling strategy.

    At its current stage, Athomic can be understood as a distributed self-adaptive scheduler whose behavior emerges from the interaction between workload characterization, adaptive feedback loops, fairness mechanisms, and distributed coordination.

    Unlike traditional schedulers, Athomic does not assume that workloads explicitly declare their resource requirements. Instead, it continuously refines workload estimations and incorporates these observations into subsequent scheduling decisions.

    This perspective shifts scheduling from a static allocation problem toward a continuously evolving decision-making process.

    6.1 Design Principles

    Athomic was not designed around a predefined theoretical framework.

    Its architecture evolved incrementally as new operational challenges emerged.

    Nevertheless, several design principles gradually became apparent and now serve as the conceptual foundation of the scheduler.

    Unified Decision Making

    Scheduling decisions should emerge from a single decision pipeline.

    Latency adaptation, fairness mechanisms, workload characterization, queue disciplines, and fleet coordination should not independently admit or reject requests.

    Instead, these components should act as evidence providers contributing to a common scheduling decision.

    Progressive Adaptation

    Resource consumption patterns should improve over time without requiring manually declared workload profiles.

    The scheduler is expected to become increasingly accurate as it accumulates execution history.

    This principle is particularly important for AI-serving systems, where requests targeting the same model may exhibit dramatically different execution characteristics.

    Distributed Awareness

    Scheduling decisions should incorporate information originating from neighboring replicas whenever possible.

    Schedulers operating in distributed environments should avoid behaving as isolated processes competing for resources without visibility into fleet-wide conditions.

    Extensibility

    Algorithms, fairness mechanisms, queue disciplines, and workload estimators should remain pluggable.

    Experimentation should not require invasive architectural modifications.

    This requirement is especially relevant for research environments where multiple scheduling strategies may coexist simultaneously.

    SLO-centric Optimization

    Athomic prioritizes Service Level Objective preservation over raw resource utilization.

    Although maximizing utilization remains desirable, preserving latency objectives is considered the primary optimization target.

    Design Tradeoff

    Distributed coordination improves fairness and fleet-wide utilization, but introduces synchronization overhead, increased implementation complexity, and potential consistency challenges.

    6.2 Scheduling Decision Pipeline

    Athomic models scheduling as a decision-making process.

    Requests are not immediately admitted or rejected.

    Instead, they progressively accumulate contextual information until enough evidence exists to make an informed decision.

    Traditional concurrency controllers optimize concurrency.

    Athomic attempts to optimize decisions.

    Architectural Observation

    Individual subsystems should not make scheduling decisions independently.

    Their role is to provide evidence that contributes to a final admission decision.

    6.3 Architectural Components

    Athomic currently consists of several cooperating subsystems.

    Although each subsystem addresses a different aspect of scheduling, none of them is intended to operate autonomously.

    Adaptive Controller

    The adaptive controller adjusts concurrency limits according to latency, throughput, and pressure signals.

    Multiple adaptation strategies may coexist, including Gradient2, AIMD-inspired algorithms, and SLO-oriented controllers.

    Cost Learner

    The Cost Learner continuously refines workload estimations based on historical execution behavior.

    Rather than requiring users to manually specify resource profiles, Athomic attempts to infer these characteristics through observation.

    Over time, this component is expected to reduce oversubscription events and improve admission accuracy.

    Pressure Fusion

    Pressure Fusion aggregates multiple telemetry streams into a unified congestion indicator.

    Potential signals include latency trends, queue depth, throughput degradation, memory pressure, and error rates.

    Combining these signals enables scheduling decisions to react to systemic behavior rather than isolated metrics.

    Queue Intelligence

    Queue Intelligence determines execution ordering and starvation prevention strategies.

    Different queue disciplines may coexist, including FIFO, LIFO, fairness-aware policies, and deadline-sensitive approaches.

    Fairness Tracker

    Fairness mechanisms attempt to preserve equitable resource allocation while remaining compatible with latency-sensitive adaptation policies.

    Current implementations are inspired by Dominant Resource Fairness, although stronger guarantees may be explored in future iterations.

    Distributed Coordination

    Distributed Coordination mechanisms share budgets, workload profiles, and fleet pressure information among replicas.

    This information enables schedulers to make decisions based on fleet-wide conditions rather than local observations.

    Research Opportunity

    Investigate whether fleet-wide implementations of Dominant Resource Fairness can preserve global fairness guarantees under eventually consistent environments.

    6.4 Scheduling Lifecycle

    Scheduling decisions inside Athomic can be interpreted as a cyclic process.

    Admission

    A request arrives and is characterized according to previously learned workload profiles.

    Evaluation

    Pressure signals, fairness constraints, queue state, and fleet-wide conditions are evaluated.

    Decision

    The scheduler determines whether the request should execute immediately, wait for additional capacity, or be gracefully rejected.

    Execution

    Accepted requests execute normally while telemetry is continuously collected.

    Learning

    Observed behavior is incorporated into workload estimations and adaptation mechanisms.

    Synchronization

    Selected information is propagated to neighboring replicas to improve future decisions.

    This lifecycle transforms scheduling into a continuously evolving feedback loop rather than a static admission problem.

    6.5 Comparison with Existing Systems

    Athomic does not attempt to replace existing schedulers.

    Instead, it occupies a previously underexplored region of the scheduling design space.

    7. Experimental Methodology

    The purpose of the experimental campaign is not simply to demonstrate that Athomic achieves higher throughput or lower latency.

    Instead, experiments are designed to investigate whether a unified scheduling architecture can simultaneously preserve Service Level Objectives, improve fairness, reduce oversubscription events, and maintain stability under heterogeneous AI-serving workloads.

    Athomic should therefore be evaluated not merely as a software implementation, but as a scheduling strategy.

    7.0 Evaluation Strategy

    The evaluation methodology is organized around four complementary hypotheses.

    First, Athomic should preserve latency objectives more effectively than traditional adaptive concurrency controllers under heterogeneous workloads.

    Second, workload learning mechanisms should reduce oversubscription events while improving overall resource utilization.

    Third, fairness mechanisms should remain effective after introducing distributed coordination.

    Finally, integrating multiple scheduling objectives into a single architecture should provide measurable benefits compared to systems optimizing only one dimension.

    These hypotheses directly correspond to the research questions introduced earlier and provide a clear mapping between scientific objectives and experimental outcomes.

    7.1 Experimental Philosophy

    Modern schedulers are frequently evaluated under highly simplified conditions.

    Experiments often assume homogeneous workloads, static resource profiles, and isolated deployments.

    These assumptions rarely hold in production AI environments.

    Inference requests may vary significantly in duration, memory footprint, token generation rates, and GPU utilization.

    Production systems are also expected to operate under failures, traffic bursts, noisy neighbors, and resource contention.

    For this reason, the evaluation methodology proposed in this document intentionally combines synthetic workloads, realistic AI-serving scenarios, and fault injection experiments.

    7.2 Benchmark Categories

    Experiments will be organized into three complementary categories.

    Synthetic Workloads

    Synthetic experiments allow precise control over workload distributions, traffic patterns, and failure conditions.

    A representative workload may consist of eighty percent short requests, fifteen percent medium requests, and five percent extremely expensive requests.

    Arrival rates will gradually increase until saturation occurs.

    AI-serving Workloads

    Several realistic services will be deployed and stressed under representative request distributions.

    Chaos Engineering

    Failures will be intentionally introduced during execution.

    Scenario Description

    Traffic Burst Sudden increase in arrival rates
    Node Failure Replica shutdown during execution
    Latency Injection Introduce network delays
    GPU OOM Simulate GPU exhaustion

    Design Principle

    Schedulers should not be evaluated exclusively under ideal operating conditions.

    Comparisons against these systems should help determine whether Athomic truly occupies a previously unexplored region of the scheduling design space.

    Metrics should characterize not only efficiency, but also service quality, fairness, stability, and resilience.

    Stability Queue Wait, Oversubscription

    Efficiency CPU, RAM and GPU Utilization

    Metrics will be continuously exported to Prometheus and stored for later statistical analysis.

    7.5 Statistical Methodology

    Each experimental configuration will be executed at least thirty times.

    Collected measurements will be analyzed using confidence intervals, effect size calculations, ANOVA, and non-parametric tests whenever distributional assumptions cannot be satisfied.

    Effect sizes should always be reported alongside p-values.

    Statistical significance alone does not guarantee practical relevance.

    7.6 Reproducibility Strategy

    Experiments should be reproducible by independent researchers.

    All workload generators, benchmark harnesses, scheduling configurations, dashboards, datasets, and analysis notebooks will be published.

    Whenever possible, experiments should be executable through a single command.

    Docker Compose and Kubernetes manifests will be provided to simplify deployment.

    Statistical analyses and figure generation scripts will also be released to facilitate independent verification.

    8. Expected Contributions

    Athomic aims to contribute along four complementary dimensions.

    Architectural Contribution

    Athomic proposes a scheduling architecture capable of combining mechanisms traditionally scattered across overload protection systems, distributed schedulers, and workload learning frameworks.

    Experimental Contribution

    The benchmark suite developed for this project may become a reusable methodology for evaluating adaptive schedulers targeting AI applications.

    Scientific Contribution

    This work seeks to provide empirical evidence regarding the advantages and limitations of integrating fairness, workload learning, distributed coordination, and adaptive admission control into a unified scheduling framework.

    Methodological Contribution

    The evaluation framework itself may become a reusable artifact for future scheduling research.

    9. Future Research Directions

    Although Athomic already incorporates adaptive concurrency mechanisms, distributed fairness, workload learning, and fleet-aware coordination, several opportunities remain unexplored.

    Fleet-wide Dominant Resource Fairness

    Current fairness mechanisms primarily operate within a single scheduler instance.

    Extending DRF semantics to fleet-wide scheduling decisions may enable stronger fairness guarantees for multi-tenant deployments.

    Predictive Admission Control

    Athomic currently reacts to observed pressure signals.

    Future iterations may investigate forecasting techniques capable of predicting congestion before saturation occurs.

    Hierarchical Scheduling

    Quota hierarchies and organizational resource boundaries may improve support for large-scale enterprise environments.

    Reinforcement Learning Assisted Scheduling

    Learning-based schedulers such as Decima suggest that adaptive policies themselves may become trainable components.

    Investigating whether reinforcement learning can improve admission decisions without sacrificing stability remains an open research question.

    Placement-aware Scheduling

    Future work may investigate affinity constraints, GPU locality, and topology-aware scheduling strategies.

    10. Threats to Validity

    Internal Validity

    Synthetic workloads may not accurately represent production traffic patterns.

    Although realistic AI-serving scenarios will be included, some behaviors observed in industrial environments may remain underrepresented.

    External Validity

    Experiments will primarily be executed using small and medium-sized language models.

    Conclusions obtained from these evaluations may not generalize to deployments involving hundreds of GPUs.

    Construct Validity

    Metrics such as Jain Fairness Index, throughput, and latency percentiles capture important scheduling properties, but may not fully represent user-perceived quality of service.

    Reproducibility

    All benchmark harnesses, workload generators, configuration files, and result datasets should be released publicly.

    11. Publication Strategy

    Athomic naturally aligns with research communities interested in distributed systems, cloud computing, and adaptive resource management.

    ACM Middleware appears particularly attractive because of its emphasis on reusable middleware systems and practical implementations.

    ACM SoCC represents another strong candidate due to its focus on cloud-native architectures and scheduling problems.

    USENIX ATC remains an aspirational target and would likely require stronger theoretical analysis and larger-scale experiments.
